Flew into Nairobi and stayed two nights in the Karen area, which felt green, quiet, and slightly removed from the intensity of the city. It’s one of those places where you wake up to birds instead of traffic. The first day was intentionally slow — just settling in, getting used to the air, the pace, and the feeling of being somewhere completely different.

Visited the Giraffe Centre that afternoon, which was honestly one of the most memorable experiences of the trip. You stand on this raised wooden platform and the giraffes come right up to you — slow, gentle, curious. Feeding them by hand felt surreal, especially seeing how calm and deliberate they are. Their tongues are unexpectedly long and a little rough, which makes the whole experience feel even more real.

Also went to the David Sheldrick Wildlife Trust early in the morning for the baby elephant feeding. Watching them run out, trunks swinging, kicking up dust, felt pure and joyful in a way that’s hard to describe. You could see their personalities — some playful, some shy — and hearing their rescue stories made the whole experience more meaningful.

Nairobi itself has this layered energy. Had dinner at a local restaurant one night — tried nyama choma (grilled meat), which is simple but really flavorful, served with kachumbari (fresh tomato and onion salad). Drank Kenyan tea, which is strong, milky, and slightly sweet — something that becomes part of your routine quickly.

On day three, flew out to the Maasai Mara National Reserve for three nights, and everything shifted. As soon as you land, it’s just open space — endless savannah, scattered acacia trees, and this quiet that feels completely different from anywhere else.

Game drives became the rhythm of each day — early mornings when the air is cool and golden, and evenings as the sun sets low over the grasslands. Saw lions lounging in the shade, elephants moving slowly in herds, giraffes crossing the horizon like silhouettes. One of the most unforgettable moments was watching a cheetah hunting — completely focused, low to the ground, moving with this quiet intensity before suddenly sprinting. It all happened fast, but the build-up was almost more powerful than the chase itself.

Also saw massive herds of wildebeest and zebras moving together, which really makes you understand how big the ecosystem is. It doesn’t feel staged at all — you’re just witnessing life happening.

The lodge itself was simple but comfortable. Meals were hearty — grilled chicken or beef, stews, rice, fresh vegetables, and always fruit like pineapple, watermelon, and passionfruit. Breakfasts often included eggs, toast, and strong coffee before heading out for drives.

Evenings were one of the best parts. Sitting around a fire after sunset, wrapped in a light jacket, listening to distant animal sounds — sometimes lions, sometimes just the wind moving through the grass. The sky at night was incredibly clear, filled with stars in a way you don’t really see in cities.

After the safari, flew to Mombasa for the last four nights, and it felt like stepping into a completely different country. The air was humid and warm, palm trees everywhere, and a strong Swahili and coastal influence in everything from the architecture to the food.

Spent time exploring Old Town Mombasa — narrow winding streets, carved wooden doors, faded pastel buildings, and a mix of Arabic, Portuguese, and African history all layered together. Visited Fort Jesus, which sits right on the water and has this heavy, historical presence. Walking through it, you can feel how many different cultures have passed through that space.

Food on the coast was completely different from Nairobi. Ate a lot of seafood — grilled fish, prawns, and octopus — usually served with coconut rice or ugali. Tried dishes cooked in coconut milk with spices that gave everything a slightly sweet, rich flavor. Fresh juices were everywhere — mango, passionfruit, pineapple — always cold and refreshing in the heat.

Finished the trip in Diani Beach, which honestly felt like a reward after everything else. White sand, bright blue water, and a much slower pace. Days were simple — swimming in the Indian Ocean, walking along the beach, watching dhow boats in the distance.

The water was warm and clear, and the tide would shift throughout the day, changing the whole landscape. Sometimes you could walk far out over shallow water, other times the waves came in stronger. Beach vendors would pass by selling handmade jewelry or coconuts, adding to the rhythm of the place.

Evenings were soft and calm — sunset over the ocean, dinners by the water, and the sound of waves in the background. Had a few cocktails like fresh coconut drinks and light rum mixes, but mostly stuck to cold beers and juice.

Kenya ended up feeling like multiple worlds in one trip — the calm greenery of Nairobi, the raw and powerful wildlife of the Maasai Mara, and then the warm, coastal stillness of Mombasa and Diani. It didn’t feel rushed, just layered — each place adding something completely different to the experience.
