Flew into Tokyo on April 3rd and stayed six days in Shinjuku, which ended up being the perfect base. The area is constantly alive — neon lights, tiny alleyways packed with restaurants, and this feeling that something is always happening no matter the time of day. Walking out of the hotel at night felt like stepping into a movie.

The first few days were mostly about exploring without a plan. Standing at Shibuya Crossing at night was one of those moments that sticks — the glow of massive screens, the sound of footsteps and chatter, and waves of people moving in every direction. I kept coming back to that area just to take it in again.

Spent a lot of time in Shinjuku itself, especially places like Omoide Yokocho — narrow alleyways packed with tiny grill spots. Smoke from the yakitori grills fills the air, and you’re squeezed into these small counters next to strangers. Ordered skewers of chicken, beef, and vegetables, all slightly charred and salty in the best way. Paired it with cold beer or a simple highball (whiskey and soda), which became one of my favorite drinks of the trip — light, crisp, and easy to keep drinking.

Shimokitazawa was one of my favorite neighborhoods overall. It felt creative and relaxed — vintage clothing shops, record stores, small coffee spots. Found a tiny café that served really smooth pour-over coffee and a simple custard dessert that ended up being one of the most memorable quiet moments of the trip.

Went to Senso-ji early in the morning, and the stillness made it feel completely different than what you usually see. The smell of incense, the soft light, and the sound of footsteps on the stone paths made it feel almost meditative. Grabbed a warm melon pan from a nearby stand afterward — slightly crisp on the outside, soft inside — which felt like the perfect simple breakfast.

Food in Tokyo honestly set the tone for the entire trip. Ramen every day, no exceptions. Each bowl felt like its own experience — some rich and creamy tonkotsu, others lighter but still deeply flavorful. The best one was that tiny 8-seat spot in Shinjuku — thick broth, perfectly chewy noodles, soft-boiled egg, and slices of pork that just melted. Sitting there shoulder-to-shoulder with strangers, quietly eating, made it feel even more authentic.

Also had some of the best sushi of my life in Ginza at a small counter where the chef served each piece directly. The fish was incredibly fresh — buttery tuna, delicate white fish, slightly sweet shrimp — and each bite was perfectly balanced. Drank sake there that was smooth and slightly fruity, completely different from what I expected.

Akihabara was more about the atmosphere than anything — bright, overwhelming, and full of energy. Grabbed random snacks while walking around — things like matcha soft serve and canned coffee from vending machines, which somehow always tasted better than expected.

Drinks became a big part of the daily rhythm. Convenience stores had surprisingly good options — iced coffee, teas, flavored drinks. Pocari Sweat became a go-to, especially after long days of walking. Nights were mostly beer (especially Sapporo), highballs, and occasionally sake. Everything felt light and easy, never too heavy.

On day seven, took the Shinkansen to Kyoto. The ride itself was incredibly smooth — quiet, fast, and comfortable. Looking out the window and seeing Mount Fuji in the distance was one of those calm, surreal moments that just sticks with you.

Kyoto felt like a completely different pace. Stayed in a ryokan in Higashiyama, which ended up being one of my favorite parts of the trip. Walking barefoot on tatami mats, wearing a yukata, and having kaiseki meals brought directly to the room made everything feel intentional and slow.

The kaiseki dinners were incredibly detailed — small dishes with different textures and flavors. Grilled fish, seasonal vegetables, delicate broths. It wasn’t just about taste, but presentation too. Drank warm sake with dinner, which felt perfect with the slower pace and quiet setting.

Early mornings in Kyoto were the best. Walking through Fushimi Inari Taisha and hiking up past the crowds, everything gradually got quieter. At the top, it was peaceful — just the sound of wind and distant city noise. The view looking out over Kyoto made the climb completely worth it.

Went to Arashiyama Bamboo Grove at sunrise, which completely changed the experience. Soft light filtering through the bamboo, almost no people, and that quiet rustling sound that makes the whole place feel surreal.

Nishiki Market became one of my favorite food stops — tried grilled mochi, which was chewy and slightly sweet, fresh tofu that was surprisingly light, and skewered seafood. Also had matcha desserts here — matcha ice cream and sweets that had a slightly bitter, earthy flavor that balanced everything out.

Last two days were in Osaka, staying in Dotonbori. The energy there is completely different — louder, brighter, more playful. The neon signs reflecting off the canal at night made it feel almost chaotic but in a fun way.

Osaka was all about food. Ate takoyaki straight from a street stand — hot, creamy inside, slightly crispy outside. Okonomiyaki was another highlight — savory, slightly sweet, covered in sauce and toppings. Everything felt indulgent but still balanced.

Also tried kushikatsu — deep-fried skewers — paired with beer. Meals here felt less formal and more about enjoyment. You could just wander, stop somewhere random, and end up having something amazing.

Visited Osaka Castle in the morning, which had great views and open space around it. Then spent the afternoon wandering covered shopping streets, stopping for snacks, drinks, and just taking everything in.

One of the best parts of the trip overall was just walking at night — especially in Tokyo and Osaka. The combination of lights, food smells, conversations, and movement made everything feel alive. Even something as simple as grabbing a late-night snack or drink felt like part of the experience.

Japan ended up being incredibly sensory — not just the big landmarks, but the small things. The taste of ramen broth, the sound of bamboo moving, the feeling of sitting in a tiny restaurant, the view of a city from above, or a quiet morning before everything wakes up.

It was the mix of all of that — food, drinks, atmosphere, and small moments — that made it feel so memorable. Definitely one of those trips where you’re already thinking about going back before it’s even over.
