Flew into Tbilisi and stayed 4 nights in the Old Town area. It’s the kind of place where you immediately feel the layers of history — Persian, Soviet, medieval Georgian — all stacked on top of each other. Stayed in a small guesthouse on a narrow hill street with balconies and carved wooden railings. Every morning started with strong coffee and khachapuri from the bakery downstairs.

First full day was mostly wandering. Walked through the old sulfur bath district in Abanotubani, where the domed bathhouses look almost like something from Central Asia. Took the cable car up to Narikala Fortress for views over the entire city — the river winding through the old rooftops and churches. Walked back down through the botanical garden which felt surprisingly lush for a city in the Caucasus.

Food in Tbilisi was ridiculously good. Ate khachapuri multiple times — the Adjarian version shaped like a boat with egg and butter melted into the cheese. Also had khinkali at a busy local restaurant where everyone was folding them by hand. Learned quickly that you’re supposed to hold the top knot, bite, and drink the broth first. Ended most evenings with Georgian wine; the country claims 8,000 years of winemaking and they still use clay qvevri buried in the ground.

Day three took a day trip north along the Georgian Military Highway toward Stepantsminda. The road climbs steadily into the Greater Caucasus Mountains. Stopped at Ananuri Fortress, a castle overlooking a turquoise reservoir. The highlight was the small church at Gergeti Trinity Church, perched dramatically above the valley with snow peaks behind it. One of the most surreal landscapes I’ve seen.

After Tbilisi took a train west to Kutaisi for two nights. Kutaisi felt quieter and more relaxed than Tbilisi. Visited Gelati Monastery, a UNESCO site with faded frescoes that date back to the Georgian Golden Age. Also explored Prometheus Cave, which has massive underground chambers and glowing blue lighting along the pathways.

From Kutaisi went into the mountains of Svaneti, staying in Mestia for three nights. The region is famous for medieval stone defense towers built by families centuries ago. Snowy peaks surround the town in every direction and it feels much more remote than the rest of the country.

Hiked out to the Chalaadi Glacier, an easy but beautiful walk through forest that suddenly opens up to ice and rock below towering mountains. Another day took a jeep up to Ushguli, one of the highest continuously inhabited villages in Europe. The stone towers there look almost unchanged from the Middle Ages.

Food in the mountains was heavier and perfect after hiking — kubdari (spiced meat bread), fresh cheese, potatoes with herbs, and homemade wine poured into huge glasses whether you asked for it or not.

Returned to Tbilisi for the final night and had one last long dinner — grilled vegetables, walnut sauces, khinkali again, and amber-colored Georgian wine. Georgia ended up being one of those trips where the landscapes, food, and history all feel completely distinct from anywhere else in Europe.
