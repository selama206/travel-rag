Mexico Trip — Sample 8–10 Day Itinerary

Flew into Mexico City and stayed four nights in Roma Norte, which ended up being the perfect home base. The neighborhood felt leafy and alive — tree-lined streets, art deco buildings, little bakeries on every corner. Every morning started with a walk to a café for a cappuccino and something light like a concha or avocado toast. It felt very local, not overly touristy, and easy to just wander without a plan.

First day was about easing in. Walked through Chapultepec Park, which is massive — more like a forest than a park. Vendors selling snacks, families out, random street performers. Made my way up to Chapultepec Castle, and the views over the city were honestly kind of surreal. You don’t realize how huge Mexico City is until you’re looking out over it like that.

Second day was more intense — headed into the historic center. The Zócalo is enormous, and everything around it feels heavy with history. Walked through the Metropolitan Cathedral, which is even more impressive inside, then wandered into side streets where things got more chaotic — street vendors, music, people everywhere. Ate some of the best tacos of the trip here — tacos al pastor shaved right off the spit, with pineapple, cilantro, onion, and a squeeze of lime. Simple but unreal. Grabbed fresh juices from a street stand too — mango and guava.

One of the highlights was a morning trip to Teotihuacan. Got there early before it got too hot. Walking down the Avenue of the Dead with the Pyramid of the Sun in front of you is one of those moments that doesn’t really feel real. Climbed partway up and just sat for a while taking it in. It’s quiet in a different way than the city — very open, very still.

Back in the city, nights were all about food. Tried different taquerías and small restaurants — esquites (corn in a cup with lime, mayo, chili), late-night churros that were crispy outside and soft inside, and mezcal at a small bar that had a smoky, almost campfire flavor. Also tried pulque one night — kind of thick and fermented, definitely an acquired taste.

After Mexico City, flew to Oaxaca and stayed three nights near the center. Completely different pace — slower, more relaxed, and incredibly colorful. Buildings in warm tones, cobblestone streets, markets full of textiles and handmade goods.

Spent a lot of time just walking without a destination. Santo Domingo Church was stunning — detailed, gold-covered interior that felt almost overwhelming in the best way. The botanical garden nearby was calm and worth a walk through.

Food in Oaxaca was on another level. Tried multiple types of mole — especially mole negro, which was rich, complex, slightly sweet, slightly spicy. Had tlayudas (kind of like a large crispy tortilla topped with beans, cheese, and meat), memelas for breakfast, and hot chocolate made with water instead of milk, which gave it a lighter but still deep flavor. Also did a mezcal tasting — learned how different each one can be depending on the agave and process.

One day took a trip out to Monte Albán. The site sits on top of a flattened mountain, and the views stretch across the entire valley. It feels more open than Teotihuacan, less crowded, and you can really take your time exploring the ruins.

Last stop was Puerto Escondido for three nights, which felt like a full reset. Everything slowed down. Stayed near the beach and spent most of the days outside.

Playa Zicatela was the main beach — huge waves, tons of surfers, not really a swimming beach but great to just sit and watch. Sunsets here were some of the best of the trip — sky turning orange and pink, people gathering along the shore.

Swam at calmer beaches nearby like Playa Carrizalillo, which required walking down a long staircase but was totally worth it. Water was clear and much easier to actually swim in.

Food here was simple but perfect — grilled fish tacos right on the beach, fresh ceviche, cold beers, and cocktails like palomas and margaritas. Everything tasted better by the ocean. Nights were low-key — beach walks, casual dinners, listening to waves.

Mexico ended up being the perfect mix — dense, energetic city life, deep history, incredible food culture, and then a complete shift into slow coastal living at the end. It felt like three different trips in one, without ever feeling rushed.
